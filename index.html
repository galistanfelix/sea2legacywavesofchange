Logline
Five years of science dedicated to one question: how do we project sea level rise in Southeast Asia accurately enough to act on it? A retrospective on SEA2, following the research from ancient coral records to sinking cities, and the communities whose futures depend on getting it right.

Content / Prompts

  • Over the course of SEA2, did your relationship with uncertainty change?
  • How do you explain uncertainty to planners or decision-makers?
  • SEA2 found that fewer than a third of Asian coastal planners account for uncertainty in their projections. What does that gap mean in practice?
Good projections need two things: a deep record of past behaviour, and a precise read of everything changing sea level today. The next two chapters build both.

  • Why was it important for SEA2 to look thousands of years into the past?
  • What do coral microatolls and mangroves allow us to see that instruments alone cannot?

04
When the Earth Moves Beneath Our Feet
Component A · Vertical Land Motion and Solid Earth · ~100 seconds
"In some cities the ground is sinking faster than the sea is rising. A twenty-year-old earthquake is still quietly making it worse."
~100 sec Interview + Archival
Visuals
InSAR deformation maps GPS stations in the field Subsidence map animations Satellite imagery Coastal megacity aerials
Profile
Grace Ng
Content / Prompts
  • In SEA2, why was it important to look at the land as well as the ocean?
  • What surprised you most when you started measuring vertical land motion across Southeast Asia?
  • How do earthquakes continue to matter long after the shaking stops?
  • The research found that the Earth's mantle beneath Southeast Asia is weaker than previously assumed. What does that change about how we model coastal risk globally?
Sinking land raises the baseline. A higher baseline means extreme events strike harder and reach further than older models predict.
